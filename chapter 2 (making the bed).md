Sunday, November 2020

Who knew what Jack was doing this morning, but his body was clearly facing the lake, whose water was crystal clear. The surface reflected Jack's face without hesitation, then fractured into ripples at the slightest tremor. Below, the fish swam as if nothing were weighing them down. It was understandable, people always said creatures like them forgot everything in minutes.

There was really no need for wabi-sabi philosophy or stoicism to simply remain indifferent. After all, why feel guilty about past mistakes if forgetting came as easily as breathing?

If only, oh, if only he could choose his second life. Surely, Jack would choose to be a small fish in an irrigation canal. Even if it stank, at least forgetting would come naturally, instead of clawing at him like something that refused to die.

But now, in his first life, he was still a machine that archived everything. Every sight, every sensation, every moment that demanded him to stand at the center of it all. Not because he possessed hyperthymesia, but because nothing inside him knew how to delete.
The wound did not close with time. It expanded.

He looked back at the lake. For a brief second, the water held another reflection. Strangely, it was not his own. He saw her face, vividly.

The laboratory air when they first met had been sterile and cold, yet somehow warmer than this morning. He once believed she was one of the few who could understand him.

He was wrong.

Now he saw her again, in memory only, carrying a stack of divorce papers across the green table. That was the last time they sat across from each other.

He leaned his body further toward the lake to see her reflection more clearly. But the surface of the water suddenly looked blurry, making it difficult for him to see her anymore. To make matters worse, a school of fish passed through his line of sight.

Crackle.

Suddenly, an idea bloomed like a poppy in his head.

He pushed himself away from the cold guardrail and placed one foot on the lower rung of the terrace. One step forward, one step higher.

In the span of a quarter of a minute, he paused. In the lake’s reflection, his memories returned one by one, from the happiest to the guiltiest, like a sin he had never committed.

"I used to be that happy, huh," Jack muttered as he recalled his happiest moments.

"But apparently not," he added as he began to recall his most cursed ones.
The water looked cold. Clear. Quiet. A place where memories would finally stop echoing. For a moment, he tried to control his breathing before he could plunge in to join the fish below.

Jack’s hand drifted toward the surface of the lake, unable to resist the coolness that flowed over the carbon of his palm. The water slid across it with a faint lotus effect. The longer his hand touched the water, the more he thought about the crazy idea he hadn't planned for a long time.

He deliberately peeled back a corner of the thin seal covering the joints of his hand beneath the water. A small glitch sparked through the system as the wires inside tasted the lake.
The seal tore with a faint stretch. Just one more pull.

For some reason, he chuckled quietly as the glitch worsened. Or perhaps, the glitch was him all along.

To experience an even sharper sensation, his hand crept to the metal covering his collarbone. He felt a functioning pulse. It was still too normal for Jack, who wanted to quickly replace his old life with a new, exciting one, like being a fish.

His fingers currently rested on the seal beneath his jaw. Just one twist.
The reflection in the lake moved. Not his. And it looked like someone with a man bun, or perhaps a ponytail.

Jack leaned closer. The metal railing slipped from beneath his weight.
For a fraction of a second, Jack felt nothing beneath his feet. The lake shattered around him.

Jack tried to move his body, forcing his feet against the water's density. He hadn't expected himself to be so nonchalant about his self-inflicted torture. He felt under his jaw once more, and it turned out there was still a film seal supporting Jack's life, something Jack himself had already given up on maintaining. He reached for the still-sticky seal and peeled it off, lifting a bit of artificial flesh.

The system was completely unable to prevent it. Finally, the thing he'd been waiting for happened.

His system began to panic, internal alarms blaring with a gloomy sound like a swarm of wasps invading his throat. The water on his hand, which had initially created only a small glitch, developed into an electrical short circuit that flickered like a lighthouse reflector.

For a few seconds, he didn't move. Not because he was exhausted, but because he was trying to enjoy the sensation.

He waited for the feeling he had imagined, trying to remain as still as the weeds swaying below. His memory had ground to a halt, and so had his system.
But instead, he felt the exact opposite.
 
The memories came rushing back.

The laboratory. 

The court. 

The stack of divorce papers... 

The smile he once trusted, a rabbit hole he had fallen into without noticing. 

It was all painful, and it hit him all at once.

As time went on, the density of the water increasingly squeezed the narrow metal gaps, drawing him deeper. His mind was almost lost, swallowed by the darkness of the lake. He tried to see everything beneath the blanket of shadows. The fish he envied, the coral, and a bit of household waste that looked like an old television with no signal, static crawling through the dark.
In the midst of Jack's successful self-destruction, and amidst the indifference of a group of fish that were already forgetting his existence, a silhouette with fingers appeared, thwarting Jack's attempt to find his second life.

...Sploosh...

It was the man whose reflection he had seen besides his wife, the ponytailed man.

"God, you're so stupid," the man complained, appearing right in front of Jack.

He was tall and slender, standing eleven centimeters taller than Jack. The ponytail on his head added another five centimeters to his height. Jack didn't lift his head to look him in the eye; instead, he lowered his gaze to avoid the man's stare, feeling smaller in comparison.
Seemingly offended, the man touched Jack's lower jaw, forcing him to look up. It hurt Jack, since the protective film had already been removed.

"Show me your puppy eyes, Blondie."

Jack tried to struggle, but because his chest was still tight with water and the pain in his jaw throbbed, he just gave up. 

Somehow, that only made the man want to mock him more.

"You were too excited to feel pain before, Jackass."

"Didn't expect you to change your entire personality a minute after trying to be a fish," he chuckled, his fingers beginning to explore Jack's neck.

Jack could have easily flung the man's hand away from the base of his neck. If he were honest, Jack didn't actually feel the slightest bit of discomfort in his heart; it was just that his neck sensor was too sensitive, not in the sense of pain, but in the sense of desire.

Yeah, he was trying to hide it.

"Ouch, my pretty boy. Trying to play hard to tame, huh?"

Jack finally dared to speak up, not to tell him to shut up, but to provoke him into saying it again.

"You always call me 'pretty boy' every time we meet. Isn't there a more creative way to fuck me in the head, boy?" Jack asked, his voice sounding almost rhythmic.

"'Cause that nickname annoyingly fits you more than goddamn 'Raiden', heh," the man chuckled again, as nonchalant as Jack's own state of mind.

Jack could only snort, playing with his breath as he heard the man laugh once more.

"I bet that was your last chuckle," he snarled through his teeth.

"Or your last bet," the man countered, refusing to lose.

Jack sighed.

He almost clicked the darker side of his button, but he didn't. He just stood there, dripping and glitching, hating himself for how much he wanted that hand to stay on his neck. Stupid, emotional, obsessive little me, he thought, as the 'pretty boy' nickname finally stopped sounding like a taunt and started sounding like something heavier, something that could stay.

He hated how he was still standing there, facing a man who felt so grossly familiar to him. He didn't know how many times he had beaten his mind to keep thinking it was a completely different figure. His brain, slightly short-circuited after contact with the water, was matching up the things he hated so much.

Of course he knew; there were two sides of his soul that were opposites, always bringing each other down. And stupidly, they both seemed to agree on each other's foolish ideas.

Oh nature, why did you create me with "Jack" in my veins? He's too stupid, too stupid for a killing machine like me. He only ever leans into it, the bloodlust. That part, at least, he never questions. And I’ve tried, God, I’ve tried to keep him in line. More times than I can count. That was roughly what was going on in Jack's mind, always haunted by his own alter-ego.

He looked as if he had tripped and fallen, even though he hadn't. He stumbled over all his words. His dual souls made things weird, and so he made them worse.

Jack felt his pulse become unstable, a wave of desire swirling around his body, but it wasn't bloodlust. He touched the man's hand that was on his neck, holding it just to feel it for a moment.

It felt like déjà vu. 

The warmth was there… but it didn’t sit right. Like something familiar wearing the wrong skin.

Jack chuckled for a moment, a little joke even though his heart was filled with an anxiety that his two souls couldn't explain.

"It's a bit crazy that someone I once defeated saved me..."

"...And I don't think that's sincere."

An inevitable moment after that sharp sentence, without warning, and without the slightest alert in Jack's nerves, the hand that once rested around his neck shifted. It struck, pinning him against the railing in one fluid, careless motion. The water below shuddered, scattering droplets into the air as if it, too, had been caught off guard.

Jack's self-defense instincts kicked in too late. Even if he were given a second to foresee the future, he wouldn't have been able to avoid it, as he couldn’t predict something that refused to follow any pattern at all. The man was immensely random, too erratic for nature itself to chart, as if the world had simply stopped trying, forcing him to weave his own sequence.

Now he was leaning on the rail, still pinning Jack down, his hands unmoving. He murmured, "Should sincerity be measured… or are you just afraid of what it might mean if it is?"

Jack just chuckled, though he looked more like prey trapped in a puma's grip now, considering how tall the man was. "Hell if I knew. You're the only one who asks me about that."

Creak...

The metal railing groaned, the frequency of the sound making Jack ache slightly. He felt like the railing separating him from the lake water would collapse at any moment, or maybe not just yet.

The hand holding him to the railing still felt heavy, still warm, disturbingly warm. He didn't think it was actually real warmth. The sound of a heartbeat could be heard, but all he could hear was the most familiar sound: his own heartbeat. What about its weight? The weight of the person pinning him? Was that also unreal? He tried to assure himself, but only gave way to the unwavering pressure of his own imagination.
This was impossible.

Jack looked at the man's face, but the pixels were starting to tear at the edges, like the household residue in the lake he had seen before. Yet, it was still too real.

"You're not here, are you?" Jack whispered, his voice cracking through the static.

The ponytailed man just grinned, a look too perfect, too 'Sam' to be real. "What a tragedy, Jackass. You're the one who made the bed. Right now you're just looking for someone to keep you from sleeping."

"You give me déjà vu," Jack confirmed the uncertainty. "And stupidly make me think two plus two equals five at the same time."

...

Jack gripped the cold railing. Under his jaw, the short circuit hummed.

He was right about the bed. He was stuck in it.

Mid-November.

Too slow.

Too cold.

But his glitching brain was already skipping pages. 

Bypassing the weeks, bending four into five.

The calendar ticked fast.

November blurred.

December bled in.
